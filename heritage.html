ANCIENT IRELAND (Before 3000 B.C.)

The Origins of Hurling

Long before written history, early forms of stick-and-ball games were played across Ireland. Archaeological discoveries and ancient folklore suggest hurling's roots stretch back thousands of years, making it one of the oldest field games still played today. Its enduring presence would become woven into Ireland's culture, mythology and identity.

HEROIC AGE (1st Century A.D.)

Setanta Becomes Cú Chulainn

Hurling sits at the heart of the Cú Chulainn legend. As a boy, Setanta's extraordinary skill with hurley and sliotar became the foundation of one of Ireland's greatest heroic tales, linking the game forever with courage and myth.

MYTHIC AGE (3rd Century A.D.)

Fionn Mac Cumhaill & The Fianna

Tales of Fionn Mac Cumhaill and the Fianna celebrate a warrior society built on strength, agility and skill. Games resembling early hurling formed part of the physical culture that prepared young warriors for hunting, travel and combat across ancient Ireland.

MEDIEVAL IRELAND (1366)

The Statute of Kilkenny

In 1366, new laws sought to restrict many native Irish customs and pastimes. The fact that traditional games were specifically mentioned highlights how important hurling had already become in Irish society and daily life.

1600s

THE GAME ENDURES (1600s)

Despite social and political upheaval, hurling survived through local communities and rural gatherings. Played in fields, meadows and village commons, the game was passed from one generation to the next, preserving a tradition that might otherwise have been lost.

THE FOUNDATION OF THE GAA (1884)

Michael Cusack & A National Revival

In 1884, Michael Cusack helped establish the Gaelic Athletic Association to preserve and promote Ireland's native games. The GAA provided hurling with a formal structure, ensuring that a sport rooted in myth, history and local tradition would thrive for future generations.

1920

Bloody Sunday

During Ireland's War of Independence period, fourteen people were shot dead at Croke Park on 21 November 1920. The tragedy became one of the defining moments in GAA history, and the Hogan Stand today commemorates Tipperary player Michael Hogan and all those who lost their lives

1950s-2000s

The Home of Gaelic Games

From All-Ireland finals to historic moments that shaped the GAA, Croke Park became the heart of hurling and Gaelic games. Its famous stands honour figures such as Michael Hogan and Michael Cusack, linking every generation of players and supporters to the sport's rich heritage.

Modern Era

Beyond Ireland

What began as an ancient Irish pastime is now played on pitches across the world. From London and New York to growing clubs throughout Europe, Australia and Asia, hurling continues to attract new players and supporters. These clubs not only preserve Irish heritage abroad but also create communities where people of different backgrounds & cultures come together through sport, friendship and shared tradition.

TODAY

A Living Heritage

Played in every generation, hurling remains one of the oldest and fastest field games in the world. From local clubs to packed All-Ireland finals, the sport continues to unite communities while carrying forward a tradition that stretches back thousands of years.
